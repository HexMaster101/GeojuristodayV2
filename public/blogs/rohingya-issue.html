    INDIA'S APPROACH TO THE ROHINGYA CRISIS: BALANCING NATIONAL SECURITY AND HUMANITARIAN CONCERNS.

    WHO ARE ROHINGYA?

    The Rohingya are an ethnic and religious minority group primarily composed of Muslims who have traditionally lived in the Rakhine State of Myanmar (formerly known as Burma). They have their own language and distinct culture, but they have faced significant persecution and discrimination in Myanmar for many years.

    The Rohingya are ethnically, linguistically, and religiously different from Myanmar’s dominant Buddhist community. They speak a dialect of Bengali, as opposed to the commonly spoken Burmese language. About 1.1 million Rohingyas are said to live in Myanmar’s Rakhine region, which is Myanmar’s least developed region, with more than 78 per cent of households living below the poverty line. Despite living in Myanmar for many generations, the Rohingya are not recognized as an official ethnic group and have been denied citizenship since 1982, making them the world’s largest stateless population. As a stateless population, Rohingya families are denied basic rights and protection and are extremely vulnerable to exploitation, sexual and gender-based violence (SGBV) and abuse. The Myanmar government has often considered them to be illegal immigrants from Bangladesh, despite many Rohingya families having lived in Myanmar for generations.

    The plight of the Rohingya gained international attention, especially in the last decade, due to widespread reports of human rights abuses and violence against them. The most significant and deadly episode occurred in 2017 when a military crackdown in Rakhine State led to a massive exodus of Rohingya refugees driving more than 742,000 people to seek refuge in neighbouring countries — the majority of whom were women and children.

    HOW DID ROHINGYA REFUGEE CRISIS BEGAN?

    In August 2017, after a massive wave of violence broke out in Myanmar’s Rakhine state, this incident was followed by, what has been described as ethnic cleansing, or even genocide by the critics. This crackdown led to the deaths of at least 10,000 Rohingyas and forced more than 742,000 people (according to UN official record) to seek refuge in Bangladesh. Entire villages were burned to the ground, Thousands of families were killed or separated and massive human right violations were reported. The United Nations has described Myanmar’s Military offensive that triggered the exodus as a “textbook example of ethnic cleansing”. Whereas Bengali Muslims in Bangladesh and India do not see the Rohingya as their kin in any respect.

    WHERE ARE THE ROHINGYA SEEKING REFUGE?

    More than 1 million Rohingya refugees have fled violence in Myanmar in successive waves of displacement since the 1990s. Now, more than 960,000 Rohingya refugees are living in Bangladesh with a majority settled in and around Kutupalong and Nayapara refugee camps in Bangladesh’s Cox’s Bazar region — some of the largest and most densely populated camps in the world. More than half of all Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh (52 percent) are children, while 51 percent are comprised of women and girls. The current refugee population accounts for one-third of the total population in the Cox’s Bazar region, making support to host communities essential for peaceful coexistence.

    Since 2021, to decongest the 33 camps in Cox’s Bazar, nearly 30,000 refugees have been relocated to Bhasan Char island by the Government of Bangladesh. While protection services and humanitarian assistance have been scaled up on the island, significant gaps remain in service delivery and the sustainability of critical assistance.

    In addition to Bangladesh, some Rohingya have sought refuge in other countries, including Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, and India. However, their situation in these countries varies, with some being recognized as refugees and others facing uncertain legal status and conditions.

    Armed clashes across Myanmar have continued to trigger displacement, bringing the total number of internally displaced people (IDP) within the country to more than 1.8 million — including 1.5 million of whom have been internally displaced since February 2021.

    HOW DOES THE MONSOON SEASON IMPACT ROHINGYA REFUGEES IN BANGLADESH?

    The Monsoon season runs from June to October each year, comes with so many challenges for the Rohingya people as it brings with itself heavy rainfall and strong winds to Bangladesh, increasing the risk of floods and landslides. Hundreds of thousands of Rohingya have found refuge in flimsy shelters made of bamboo and tarp which have been built in areas prone to landslides, which may not stand torrential rains and heavy winds. Cox's Bazar, while providing temporary refuge for many Rohingya, is not a safe place for them to live for several reasons.

    The Cox's Bazar region is prone to natural disasters, including cyclones, monsoons, flooding, and landslides. These disasters can lead to loss of life, displacement, and destruction of shelters, making it an unsafe environment for refugees living in makeshift camps. The refugee camps in Cox's Bazar, especially Kutupalong-Balukhali, are severely overcrowded. The high population density can lead to unsanitary conditions, limited access to basic services, and increased risks of disease outbreaks. While humanitarian organizations provide essential services to refugees in the camps, the large and growing population of refugees can strain the capacity to deliver healthcare, education, clean water, and sanitation services. This can result in inadequate access to these critical services. There have been reports of security concerns within the camps, including incidents of violence and criminal activity. These issues can make the living environment less safe for Rohingya refugees, particularly women and children. Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh often lack formal legal status and citizenship, leaving them in a state of vulnerability. They are at risk of deportation and may not have access to education, formal employment, or the ability to move freely. The harsh conditions and experiences in the refugee camps can have severe psychological and emotional impacts on the Rohingya, particularly those who have experienced trauma in Myanmar. Mental health support is limited, making it difficult for refugees to cope with their circumstances. The camps in Cox's Bazar were initially set up as a temporary solution, but the protracted nature of the Rohingya crisis has meant that many refugees have been living there for years without clear prospects for a better future.

    HOW IS ROHINGYA CRISIS AFFECTING CHILDREN?

    While basic services have been provided, children still face disease outbreaks, malnutrition, inadequate educational opportunities and the risks related to neglect, exploitation and violence including gender-based violence risks, child marriage and child labour. Meanwhile, annual cycles of heavy monsoon and cyclones pose substantial risks to both Rohingya refugees and host communities. Rohingya children in Rakhine State, have been hemmed in by violence, forced displacement and restrictions on freedom of movement.

    Older children and adolescents who are deprived of opportunities to learn or make a living are at real risk of becoming a “lost generation,” ready prey to traffickers and those who would exploit them for political or other ends. Girls and women are at particular risk of sexual and other gender-based violence in this situation, including being forced into early marriage and being left out of school as parents keep them at home.

    HOW IS INDIA HANDLING THE ISSUE?

    India's stance on the Rohingya issue reflects a complex balancing act between its national security concerns, geopolitical interests, domestic considerations, and its humanitarian responsibilities. The issue highlights the challenges that countries face when dealing with refugee crises and the need for a nuanced and comprehensive approach that considers both security and humanitarian aspects.

    India have been recieving Rohingya refugees and allowing them to settle in the different parts of the country over the years, especially after the communal voilence in the state of Rakhine. However, India considers the refugee crisis as an internal affair of Myanmar. India also believes that ASEAN has an undeniable responsibility to resolve this crisis. Since India is not a signatory to the United Nations Convention on Refugees, refugee status granted by the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) to the Rohingya was irrelevant to their deportation.

    India is home to about 40,000 Rohingya refugees. Of these approximately 16,000 are registered with the UN Refugee Agency. While the United Nations has called upon the Indian government to provide Rohingya with the necessary protection, India maintains that they are a threat to its national security and have links with international terror groups. In India, the term ‘Rohingya’ is not used in public statements or documents. Citizenship (Amendment) Bill, 2016 further cleared the government’s stand, it provided that :


    The Bill aims to amend the 1955 Citizenship Act, to provide citizenship to illegal migrants from Afghanistan, Bangladesh and Pakistan, who are of Buddhist, Hindu, Jain, Sikh, Parsi or Christian.

    India deported a few Rohingya refugees back to Myanmar in October 2018 by handing over them to Myanmar authorities. India has abstained from voting on a resolution backed by Bangladesh in United Nations Human Right Council (UNHRC), criticizing the Myanmar government over its dealing with Rohingya crisis. It has displeased the Bangladeshi government. The Supreme Court of India has asked Union Government to strike a balance between humanitarian concern for Rohingya community staying in India and country’s economic and national security interests. SC has refused to release 300 Rohingyas from detention camps in Jammu and Delhi. The court called for their deportation under the Foreigners Act, 1946.

    PROVISION PROTECTING REFUGEES IN INDIA.

    The Supreme Court of India held that Refugees cannot invoke Article 32 of Indian Constitution. However, the Constitutional safegaurd of Article 14 and 21 are equally available to every person including refugees. While, other fundamental rights are only available to the citizens of India. Whereas, Article 51 of the constitution mandates the state to promote international peace and security. The Supreme Court of India did not comment on the genocide that is happening in Myanmar. Though India is non-signatory to the refugee convention 1951 or its 1967 protocol, India is bound by the principle of Non-Refoulement. Principle of Non-Refoulement is binding on all UN member states that does not allow any country to return people who have sought refuge within their borders, to a country in which there is high likelihood that they would face persecution based on race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion. Under Article 14 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, anyone can seek asylum in another country from persecution.

    ISSUES AND CONCERNS TO INDIA'S SECURITY.

    Though India has been receiving Rohingya refugees and allowing them to settle in the different parts of the country over the years but India expressed concerns that some Rohingya refugees might pose security risks, as they could potentially be vulnerable to radicalization by extremist groups. The Indian government believed that they could be used by militant organizations to further their agendas. As a result, India viewed the presence of Rohingya refugees as a potential national security threat. India shares a border with Myanmar and has been keen on maintaining good relations with the Myanmar government. Myanmar is seen as a strategic partner in India's "Act East" policy and in countering Chinese influence in the region. India has sought to maintain friendly ties with Myanmar's military, which has been criticized for its treatment of the Rohingya population. India's foreign policy decisions are often influenced by these geopolitical considerations. While India has expressed concern about the situation of the Rohingya, its response has been more focused on providing humanitarian assistance to the community, rather than offering asylum. India has contributed aid to support Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh and provided assistance to internally displaced Rohingya in Myanmar's Rakhine State. India has contended that the Rohingya refugees do not have legal status in the country, and it has sought to deport those living in India back to Myanmar. This approach has faced legal challenges, with human rights groups and activists arguing that deporting Rohingya refugees to Myanmar would expose them to the risk of persecution.

    CURRENT LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK IN INDIA TO HANDEL REFUGEES.

    Foreigners Act of 1946: Under Section 3 of this act the central government is empowered to detect, detain and deport illegal foreign nationals.

    Registration of Foreigners Act of 1939: Under this, there is a mandatory requirement under which all foreign nationals (excluding overseas citizens of India) visiting India on a long-term visa (more than 180 days) are required to register themselves with a Registration Officer within 14 days of arriving in India.

    Passport (Entry into India) Act, 1920: Under Section 5, authorities can remove an illegal foreigner by force under Article 258(1) of the Constitution of India.

    Citizenship Act, 1955: It provided provisions for renunciation, termination, and deprivation of citizenship.

    Further, Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019 (CAA) seeks to provide citizenship to Hindu, Christian, Jain, Parsi, Sikh, and Buddhist immigrants persecuted in Bangladesh, Pakistan, and Afghanistan.

    STRATEGY FOR THE FUTURE.

    Although positive steps have been taken by India, it's notable that all these developments began soon after China stepped in with its “three-step solution” to the Rohingya crisis and did not compel anyhow to Myanmar to take any reformative steps towards the rehabilitation of Rohingya. India is concerned that raising the issue publicly might push Myanmar towards China. China has entered in almost all neighbouring countries of India. India also has a connectivity interest to link its landlocked northeastern region with the Bay of Bengal through Rakhine State under a joint project with Myanmar that includes port at Sittwe, Kaladan River and road construction in North-East. India is suffering from cross-border ethnic insurgency from Myanmar based militants, thus it requires the support of the Myanmar military to deal with them. India needs to ensure that no Rohingya refugee in India is deported back to Myanmar until it is safe to do so. Like any nation, India has the right to prioritise its national security. However, security concerns need to take into account the comprehensive long-term consequences. Moreover, given its historical bent towards protecting refugees, India must rise to the occasion and demonstrate that it is not driven solely by narrow domestic political interests. In spite of not being a party to the 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol, India has been one of the largest recipients of refugees in the world. Therefore, if India had Domestic legislation regarding refugees, it could have deterred any oppressive government in the neighbourhood from persecuting their population and making them flee to India.
